Becoming She - The true nature of what is required to transition (12/06/2026)

Nicolette S.James and David Katan

Tess Lapointe is a retired real estate agent from Oregon, where she ran her own firm with quite a few brokers working for her. Now, at the age of 70, she has bought a place and currently lives here in Salento. She was at the Berkeley to talk about, and read us extracts from, her recently published book, an autobiography of her life. The event was enthralling to say the least, given the intimate details of her life and change that she happily let us into. The change is in the title of the book “Becoming She”, and the entire evening focussed on just that.

She, of course is an identifying pronoun, which categorically marks the person referred to as female and not male. And this was the topic of the evening. Tess explained that immediately after her transitioning to a woman, it would upset her when people referred to her as him. However, she soon reasoned that the transitioning was based on her own personal needs and that she could not expect others to easily step in line with a transition that had taken her a lifetime to achieve. Also, she ruefully accepted, that like most of us, if we’re thinking of changing the way our body looks, our go-to ideal would be clearly good-looking and ideally around 25. Tess, however, did not begin to transition till she was over 60, so had spent all those years developing the physique and voice of a man. Things were not helped as Tess was endowed with big feet, “not much of a butt”, and not a great hairline. She managed to exacerbate nature’s rather unwelcome gifts by spending much of her working life outdoors in construction and other manual labour jobs. So, although she has spent eye-watering sums on everything from full scale gender-affirming surgery: top and bottom surgery and facial surgery, Tess is fully aware that her form, though feminine is not pure female. So, pronouns are not a major concern.

Indeed, this was an important she made during the evening. We are all part blue and part pink. This Tess knew when she was called Chris age just 6, and she found that things pink were more attractive than things blue, though she kept this understanding about herself fully hidden even to her own family. How it then took over 50 years to normalise her identity was what drove the talk. She began the story by reading the opening chapter to her book. Here, Chris is going through a tempestuous adolescence. His family is a respectable affluent Catholic family, His parents are hardworking, while Chris himself, wedged in the middle of 6 siblings, unsurprisingly perhaps is the black sheep of the family.

The chapter opens with Chris in his basement room at 15, already high on pot and enjoying the cranked-up sounds of Jimi Hendrix. It’s freezing temperature outside, but soon he’s out of the house, taking not one but 4 tabs of acid with a group of mates. Fairly standard business apparently for late 1960s suburban kids trying to escape the more conservative lifestyles imposed by their parents. Next morning, his father is taking him after 3 hours sleep for the dreaded haircut. Thankfully, conversation--as per the norm--was minimal. But something snapped, and Chris with no more than a couple of dollars, some weed (obviously) and a thin army-surplus jacket, starts hitchhiking – towards anywhere as long as its warm and is a long way from home. He manages to survive for two weeks before being returned home.

We hear very little about life until he meets the love of his life. A gorgeous woman, Carine. Tess made it absolutely clear that his/her feelings have not changed. Tess still likes women – and still loves Carine and their adult daughter. Indeed, the next morning after the talk Tess was off to Florence to holiday with her daughter. But Tess’s wife, unfortunately, though extremely supportive in this extraordinarily dramatic tale also still prefers men. Given it took 4 years for Tess to begin to open up to his wife, one can imagine that when the time came for the transition, marriage would be tricky. In the end, Carine and daughter moved to Holland, where Carine’s family is from. This was part of the reason why Tess also chose to move to Europe, to be closer to them.

In much of her talk Tess told us about the problems connected to vocabulary, in the 70s there was no such word as Transgender, only Transvestite. In order to discover things about her situation she spent time in the public library, often having to hide the books she was consulting. There was no LGBT+ community, and certainly not in the conservative smalltown America that was home to Tess.

The USA today has many states who persecute transgender people and with the present Trump government laws are being passed to make it illegal for teens to have access for treatment. Tess mentioned, for example, that if puberty blockers had been available she would have gladly made use of them. Her main point throughout the talk is that transgender is not a fad, but that gender is a reality one is born with and may not match your biological sex. Indeed, it was absolutely clear that no one would ever wish to become transgender on a whim. Tess went through a mandatory two year cycle of expensive psychotherapy, well before undergoing two excruciatingly painful operations, spending north of one hundred thousand dollars, then to face a community dominated by Pentecostal Christians who condemned transgender people, not to mention losing her marriage.

Thus she spent most of her life hiding her real identity, but finally took the decision at the age of 62. Support was surprisingly strong. Her daughter was fully behind her transition, and many of her employees and friends had absolutely no problem with it. However, there is an increasingly transphobic atmosphere in the USA at present with individual states legislating on ‘his/her and blue/pink. Distinctions based exclusively on biological sex are making it difficult for transgender people to exist in public. Tess pointed out that it is much easier to get angry with a minority group than to work on important issues. Less than 1% of the US population is transgender, yet trans issues have become a central culture war topic, allowing politicians to not focus on more difficult topics such as education, health or housing.

Tess also informed us that the situation may be worse in other countries. There are 13 countries, for example, where a transgender person can be put to death, and over 60 where they can be attacked, imprisoned and possibly killed with impunity. She ended the talk with her move to Salento. While it is still “Europe” it is also a long way from her frozen Oregon, and is also close to her own mother’s roots in Apricena, Northern Puglia. She has been here a couple of years and has found a warm and welcoming community, in what should be a country dominated by a straight-laced Catholic culture. Indeed, one of her first friendships was with her next-door neighbour, an elderly retired nun.

Her talk was extremely informative, and also full of humour and understanding. It helped all of us understand something of what it means to need to become she.
